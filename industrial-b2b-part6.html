Section 1 — The Foundation

Retention Is the Business

Acquiring a client is expensive. Losing one is catastrophic — not just financially, but in momentum, morale, and the credibility that comes from sustained results. In industrial B2B, where relationships compound over time, retention is where the real value is built.

The Math That Makes This Non-Negotiable

OBx's target is >96% account retention and >96% MRR retention — measured monthly. That target exists because we know the math: a retained client with a growing relationship is exponentially more valuable than the constant treadmill of replacing churned accounts. Retention isn't just good for clients. It's the financial engine of the business.

And it gets worse. The cost isn't just the lost revenue. It's the acquisition cost to replace them, the ramp-up time to rebuild momentum with a new client, the team morale hit, and the reputational impact if the departure wasn't handled well. Losing one industrial client costs you far more than the last invoice they paid.

📈

Retention Drives Growth

A retained client with an expanding relationship is your best growth story. Cross-sells, service expansions, and budget increases all start with trust — which starts with consistent performance and consistent communication.

🔁

Churn Creates a Treadmill

When accounts churn at even 5–8%, you spend all of your growth just staying flat. Every churned account requires a new client just to break even — before you can actually grow. Retention is the multiplier on every new business win.

🤝

Industrial Relationships Compound

In industrial B2B, long-term relationships mean deeper knowledge of the client's business, stronger ICP alignment, and more intelligent strategy over time. The longer you work with an industrial client, the better you get at driving results for them. Don't give that up.

Section 2 — The Tool

What Is Heartbeat?

Heartbeat is OBx's client health monitoring system. It translates the quality of your client relationships and account management behaviors into a quantitative score — giving you a real-time view of where every client stands, before a problem becomes a conversation you weren't prepared for.

What Heartbeat Measures

  • How consistently you're communicating with the client (voice, email, meetings)
  • How well you're delivering on your commitments (plan adherence, request fulfillment)
  • Whether reports are submitted on time — the most basic accountability signal
  • Client satisfaction as measured by NPS scores
  • Budget health — are they maintaining, growing, or declining investment?
  • Whether service line changes or deactivation requests have occurred

Why It Matters

  • Without it, at-risk accounts often go undetected until a cancellation notice arrives
  • It creates accountability — account health is visible to the team, not just the IC
  • It quantifies things that are often treated as subjective — "how is the relationship?"
  • It catches patterns early — declining score over 3 weeks is a signal, not a surprise
  • It aligns the entire team around what "doing the job well" actually looks like day to day
  • It keeps client interactions from being reactive — you manage proactively because the score is always visible
Think of It This Way

If Heartbeat is showing a client at 68 — that's not a scoring system telling you you're failing. That's an early warning system telling you to act before the client starts looking elsewhere. Every point in the score is a behavior. Change the behaviors and the score — and the relationship — changes with it.

Section 3 — The Score

The Health Score: Ranges, Meaning, and What to Do

Health is scored on a scale of 0–100. Every account falls into one of three zones — and each zone carries a different expectation for how you respond. Know your zones.

85–100
✅ Good
The relationship is healthy. Communication is consistent, deliverables are on track, and the client is satisfied. Your job here is to maintain and expand — not coast.
  • Keep communication cadence strong — don't let it drift
  • Look for cross-sell opportunities — healthy clients are expansion candidates
  • Proactively share wins and positive signals before the monthly report
  • Explore deeper strategy conversations and QBRs
70–84
⚠️ Needs Attention
Something in the relationship has slipped. The score is declining — and if not addressed, it will move into At Risk territory. Act now, not at the next check-in.
  • Identify which factor(s) dropped the score — communication? Reporting? NPS?
  • Schedule a direct check-in call — don't just send an email
  • Acknowledge any slippage before the client has to bring it up
  • Create a specific action plan to recover the score within 30 days
0–69
🔴 At Risk
The relationship is in jeopardy. This requires immediate attention and escalation. An At Risk account can churn — and OBx's target is fewer than 8 At Risk accounts at any time.
  • Escalate immediately — this requires team and leadership awareness
  • Schedule an urgent client meeting — voice or video, not email
  • Conduct a root cause analysis — what went wrong and when?
  • Build a formal recovery plan with milestones and leadership sign-off
Performance Standard

An account is considered positively performing if it meets 2 out of 3 criteria: (1) pacing ahead in Traffic YoY/YTD, (2) pacing ahead on Revenue/Leads YoY/YTD, (3) growing in Share of Voice. If Revenue/Leads is negative — it's an automatic negative, regardless of other metrics. If performance drops below 80% for 3 consecutive weeks, it must be raised with the broader team. Your target: over 80% of assigned accounts with positive performance.

Section 5 — Non-Negotiable

Interactions Are Table Stakes — Not Optional

The most common driver of declining Heartbeat scores isn't bad strategy or missed results. It's a drift in communication frequency. This section is direct: if you're not touching the client regularly, you're not doing the job.

The Reality

Industrial clients are busy. They're running production floors, managing supply chains, and dealing with real operational pressure. When your communication drifts, they don't assume things are going well. They start wondering if anything is happening at all. Silence creates narratives — and in the absence of information, clients tell themselves a story. Make sure you're the one writing that story by staying in contact.

📞
Phone Check-In Call
+15 pts · At least 1 per 31 days
A 15-minute check-in call is worth more than a long report that goes unread. Phone calls signal you're attentive, you're available, and you care how things are going — not just in the metrics. For industrial clients who are often more comfortable talking than emailing, calls are especially high-value.
💻
Video Meeting
+20 pts · Highest single communication score
Video meetings build the most trust per interaction because they're personal, focused, and demonstrate commitment of time on both sides. Monthly strategy reviews, QBRs, and kickoffs should always be video. Even a brief mid-month check-in on video signals seriousness.
📧
Email Communication
+5 pts each · Separate from reports
Regular email communication — sharing a quick win, flagging an opportunity, acknowledging a concern — keeps the relationship warm between formal touchpoints. This is distinct from sending reports. A "wanted to share something I noticed in your account this week" email is worth 5 points and significant goodwill.
📊
Monthly Report — By the 15th
+15 pts · Late reports penalized
The monthly report is the most visible deliverable you own. Submitting by the 15th signals professionalism and accountability. Late reports — especially after EOM — send the opposite signal. The report is often what the client shows their leadership team. Make it count, and make it on time, every time.
🤝
In-Person Meeting
+20 pts · Same as video
In industrial B2B especially, face-to-face relationships matter enormously. If you have the opportunity to visit a client on-site — to see their facility, meet the team, and understand their operation firsthand — take it. Nothing accelerates trust like showing up in person. It's also one of the best ways to generate Loop-relevant ICP insight.
⭐
NPS Response
Up to +10 pts · Detractor = −10 pts
NPS is your direct signal of how the client feels. A detractor (≤5) is a client who will tell others they're unhappy — proactive relationship repair is urgent. A passive (7–8) is a client who could go either way. A promoter (>9) is a client you should ask for a testimonial and look to expand. Don't wait for bad NPS to have a retention conversation.
Section 6 — Catch It Early

Retention Warning Signs — Catch Them Before They Escalate

Most client churn doesn't happen overnight. It builds over weeks and months through patterns that are visible — if you're paying attention. Here are the signals that tell you a relationship needs immediate attention.

⚠️ Early Warning Signs — Act Proactively

📉Communication Frequency Is Dropping
Meetings are being rescheduled, emails are going days without a response, and you're doing most of the outreach. Act immediately. A client going quiet is a client who is either frustrated or disengaged — neither is good. Schedule a direct check-in call and use ELFEC to open the conversation honestly.
😶Short, Disengaged Responses in Meetings
The client used to ask questions and engage. Now they say "sounds good" to everything and seem distracted. They've mentally checked out or are evaluating alternatives. This is the time for an honest ELFEC-structured conversation — start with Listen and create real space for them to share what's changed.
🔍Requests for Detailed Competitive Analysis
Suddenly they want to know what competitors are doing and what other agencies charge. This often means they're in evaluation mode. Respond by leaning into your value story — bring specific results data, Loop/CallRail insights, and a forward-looking strategy conversation that reminds them what they'd be giving up.

🔴 High-Alert Signs — Escalate Immediately

💸Budget Reduction Request or Pause
A budget decrease is one of the most significant negative signals in the Health Score (−15). Don't let this conversation happen without a direct meeting first. Understand the driver — financial constraint, performance concern, or change in strategy — and address it head-on with data and a specific plan.
❌Service Line Deactivation Request
This is a formal at-risk signal. The client has made a decision to reduce scope. Escalate within the team immediately, schedule an urgent retention conversation, and conduct a root cause analysis. What happened, when did it start, and what can be done? Come to the conversation prepared with a concrete recovery plan.
📊Negative Performance for 3+ Consecutive Weeks
If an account is underperforming on Revenue/Leads for 3 consecutive weeks, it must be raised with the broader team — this is policy, not optional. Underperformance that isn't proactively addressed becomes a retention crisis. Bring a specific action plan, set a clear expectation timeline, and communicate directly with the client about what you're doing about it.
Section 7

The Quarterly Portfolio Review

Every 90 days, accounts are formally reviewed for health, performance, and risk. The Quarterly Portfolio Review (QPR) is where retention is proactively managed at a team level — not just individually. Here's how it works and why it matters.

1

At-Risk Account Spotlight — 25-Minute Block

Each QPR dedicates focused time to 2–3 accounts with underperformance or client dissatisfaction. This isn't a blame session — it's a structured, team-level problem-solving conversation. Who knows this client? What do we know about the root cause? What's the plan?

2

Root Cause Discussion

For each at-risk account, identify the specific cause: revised content plans, technical audits, communication gaps, performance issues, or a shift in client expectations. Name it specifically — vague root causes lead to vague solutions.

3

Corrective Action Plan

Every at-risk account reviewed should leave the QPR with a specific, time-bound corrective action plan. Who owns what, by when, and what does success look like in 30 and 60 days? Apply the "Who Will Do What By When" framework.

4

Preparation: What to Bring

Review client sentiment, recent communication history, NPS scores, and QoQ/YoY performance trends before the QPR. Heartbeat's health history and account field data are your primary inputs. The more prepared you are, the more productive the conversation.

5

The Goal: Prevent the Conversation You Weren't Prepared For

The QPR exists so that churn doesn't come as a surprise. Every churn event that could have been prevented with earlier intervention is a failure of proactive management — not just of delivery. The QPR is the system that closes that gap.

Key Benefits of the QPR

Proactive support builds long-term trust and client loyalty. Aligned expectations reduce the misunderstandings that lead to churn. Early issue detection pinpoints underperformance before it escalates into a cancellation notice. These three outcomes are why the QPR is a non-negotiable part of how OBx manages client health.

Section 8 — Vertical Context

Retention in Industrial B2B: Why It's Different Here

Everything you've learned about Heartbeat and client health applies across all clients. But industrial B2B clients have specific retention dynamics that are worth knowing before you find out the hard way.

🏭 The Industrial Client's Retention Mindset

  • Industrial clients have long memories — they remember what you promised in the kickoff
  • They are skeptical of digital marketing until they see real results — patience is required but results matter enormously
  • Lead quality concerns can surface months into a program once the sales team weighs in
  • They often don't proactively give feedback — going quiet doesn't mean things are fine
  • They make decisions slowly — but once they decide to leave, it's usually final
  • They talk to peers in the industry — your reputation with one industrial client can influence others

🔑 Retention Behaviors That Work in This Vertical

  • Bring industry intelligence proactively — search trends, competitor movements, new keyword opportunities
  • Use Loop + CallRail data to show you understand their buyers at a level they don't have internally
  • Connect wins back to business outcomes in their language — not digital marketing jargon
  • Acknowledge the long sales cycle early and set realistic expectations about attribution timelines
  • Ask about sales team feedback on lead quality at every monthly review — close the loop
  • Educate them on the value of what we're building — organic rankings compound; stopping too early means losing the investment

Account Performance Standards — Know What You're Being Measured Against

✅ Positively Performing

Meets 2 of 3 criteria

  • Pacing ahead in Traffic (Users/Sessions) YoY/YTD
  • Pacing ahead on Revenue/Leads YoY/YTD
  • Growing in Share of Voice

❌ Negatively Performing

Revenue/Leads = automatic negative

  • Revenue/Leads negative = automatic negative regardless of other metrics
  • Only 1 of 3 performance criteria met
  • Performance below 80% for any period

🚨 Escalation Required

Mandatory team raise

  • Performance drops below 80% for 3 consecutive weeks
  • Issue must be raised with broader team — this is policy
  • Target: >80% of assigned accounts positively performing at all times

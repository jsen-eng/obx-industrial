Before We Start

The Mindset Reset

The most important thing to understand before we get into anything else.

💡 Core Principle

You do not need to be an expert in your client's industry to do excellent work for them. A very common hang-up for people new to this vertical is feeling like they need to understand die casting, steel processing, hydraulics, or machining inside and out. You don't. Industrial businesses are complex — and that's exactly why most of them have weak digital presences. Your job is to know enough to differentiate them online, speak the right language, and attract the right buyers. That's it.

🎯

What You Do Need

  • What type of business they are (manufacturer, distributor, supplier)
  • Who their buyers are and how those buyers search
  • Their key differentiators — speed, certifications, materials, geography
  • The language and terminology of the industry
  • How to apply B-SMART® to build keyword strategy
  • How to quickly read a client's website and intake form
🚫

What You Don't Need

  • How to operate a CNC machine or press
  • The exact chemical composition of every alloy
  • How to read engineering blueprints or GD&T tolerances
  • Every detail of their manufacturing process
  • Pricing knowledge for industrial products
  • Years of experience in an industrial field
🔑

Your Competitive Edge

Industrial companies are often significantly behind in digital marketing — weak SEO, minimal PPC, outdated websites. The bar to make a real impact is lower than you think. If you understand the fundamentals and speak the language, you will win for these clients.

Also Part of the Mindset Reset

Industrial B2B Is Not eCommerce — And It Should Never Be Treated Like It

One of the most common early missteps in this vertical is applying eCommerce-style, transactional language to industrial B2B clients. These are fundamentally different business models with different buyers, different buying processes, and different conversion events entirely.

⚠️ Critical Mindset Shift

Industrial buyers are not browsing an online store and making impulse decisions. They are solving complex operational challenges, sourcing materials to exact specification, and managing large-scale procurement processes that involve formal quotes, multi-stakeholder approvals, compliance documentation, lead time coordination, and supplier relationships that may span years. Transactional language like "Shop Now," "Buy Online," "Add to Cart," or "Order Today" signals the wrong business model entirely — and it will erode trust with the very buyers you're trying to reach.

❌ Transactional / eCommerce Language — Avoid
These terms signal an eCommerce model that most industrial companies are not set up for
  • "Shop Now" or "Shop Our Catalog"
  • "Buy Online" or "Buy Now"
  • "Add to Cart" or "Add to Bag"
  • "Order Today" or "Order Online"
  • "Free Shipping on Orders Over $X"
  • "Limited Time Offer — Sale Ends Sunday"
  • "Checkout" used as a primary CTA
  • Flash sale or discount urgency language
✅ Intent-Based / B2B Language — Use This Instead
These terms match how industrial buyers think, search, and take action
  • "Request a Quote" or "Get a Quote"
  • "Submit an RFQ"
  • "Contact Us for Pricing"
  • "Talk to a Specialist" or "Speak with an Engineer"
  • "Download Spec Sheet" or "View Certifications"
  • "Call for Availability" or "Check Lead Times"
  • "Get a Custom Quote" or "Request a Sample"
  • "Let's Discuss Your Project"

Why Industrial Companies Aren't Set Up for eCommerce

Transactional keywords and eCommerce CTAs fail in this vertical for very specific structural reasons — not just preference. Understanding the why makes you a smarter strategist on day one.

💰

Pricing Is Never Standard

Industrial pricing is quote-based, volume-dependent, and often tied to ongoing contract terms. There is no fixed price to display on a product page — there's a conversation to start. "Add to Cart" has nowhere meaningful to lead.

📋

The Buying Process Is Formal

Industrial purchases involve RFQs, purchase orders, supplier vetting, compliance reviews, and multi-stakeholder sign-off. This is not a checkout flow — it's a procurement process that can span weeks or months.

🔩

Products Are Highly Specific

Buyers need exact grades, tolerances, certifications, and custom configurations. You can't select "6061-T6 aluminum bar, 1.5" OD, tolerance ±0.001", ASTM B211 certified" from a dropdown. It requires a real conversation.

🤝

Relationships Drive Repeat Business

Most industrial suppliers win and retain business through relationships, reliability, and account management — not loyalty programs or discount codes. The sales model is fundamentally relationship-based, not cart-based.

⚖️

Compliance & Risk Are Part of Every Decision

In aerospace, defense, medical, and regulated industries, sourcing decisions carry compliance and liability implications. Buyers cannot simply "click and buy" — they need documentation, certifications, and full traceability records first.

📌

The Bottom Line

Transactional language doesn't just fail to convert industrial buyers — it signals that you don't understand their business. Matching your language and CTAs to their procurement reality is a trust signal in itself. It immediately communicates that you know how this industry works.

Section 1

What Is B2B Lead Generation & Why It Matters

B2B lead generation is the process by which a business attracts other businesses as potential buyers for their products or services. The focus is specifically on targeting professionals who have a need on behalf of their organization — not personal purchases.

1

Identify the ICP

Build Ideal Customer Profiles based on company size, industry, pain points, purchase urgency, and decision-making structure. Know who you're trying to reach before anything else.

2

Attract Clients

Via inbound channels (SEO, PPC, UX/CRO, content) and outbound (cold calling, trade shows, direct email). In industrial B2B, inbound is a major growth lever most clients underutilize.

3

Capture & Nurture

Engage prospects with relevant content, a strong web presence, and timely follow-up. In B2B, nurturing is critical — most buyers don't convert on the first visit.

4

Qualify Leads

Determine readiness to buy. Classify leads as MQLs (Marketing Qualified) or SQLs (Sales Qualified) depending on where they are in the purchase cycle.

5

Sales Handoff

Qualified inbound leads are assigned to the client's sales team to build relationships, issue quotes, and finalize the deal. Our job is to get them to the door — sales gets them across the threshold.

💡

Why This Matters for You

Understanding this flow helps you contextualize your work. A form fill, a phone call, a quote request — these are the outputs you're optimizing for. Every keyword, ad, and content piece should connect back to moving someone through this process.

Section 2

How B2B/Industrial Marketing Differs

B2B transactions are fundamentally different from B2C — and industrial B2B has additional layers of complexity that shape everything about your strategy.

1

Businesses Are the Buyers

The main buyers are other companies — procurement teams, engineers, plant managers — not individual consumers making personal choices.

2

The Nature of Purchases

Volume, frequency, and order value are vastly different. A single B2B customer can represent hundreds of thousands — or millions — of dollars over time.

3

Additional Complexity

Supply chain requirements, lead times, compliance standards, customization needs, and urgency risk add layers of complexity not seen in consumer marketing.

4

Longer Sales Process

Longer sales cycles, formal proposals and contracts, multiple stakeholders, and post-sale support are standard. Don't expect quick conversions the way you would in eCommerce.

Section 3

The Nature of B2B Purchases & Lead Value

One of the most important mental shifts when working with industrial B2B clients is understanding that a single lead can be worth an enormous amount — not just in the immediate transaction, but over a long relationship.

📦

Large Orders

Quantities are typically higher in volume and price. A product with a $10.00 price point, ordered at 10,000 units, becomes a $100,000 order. Volume transforms the math entirely.

🔄

Order Frequency

Frequency multiplies order volume. If a business needs 10,000 units every 6 months for 2 years, that single customer relationship becomes a $400,000 customer — from one form fill.

🤝

Long-Term Relationships

The complexity of industrial procurement leads to ongoing contracts, blanket POs, and supplier-buyer relationships that last years. Switching costs are high — once you win, you tend to keep winning.

Lead Evaluation Formulas

Lifetime Value of a Customer (LTV)

Formula

(Avg. Purchase Value × Purchase Frequency) × Avg. Customer Lifespan

Example: $2,500/month × 12 months × 2 years = $60,000 initial customer value

Lead Comparison — Same Product, Different Context

One-time buyer

$10 × 1,000 units × 1 time = $10,000

Recurring buyer

$10 × 1,000 units × 2×/year × 3 years = $60,000

Section 4 — Enhanced

The Industrial Buyer — Who They Are & How They Buy

Industrial buyers are not impulse purchasers — they are methodical, spec-driven, and risk-averse. Understanding who they are changes how you approach every content and campaign decision.

⚙️
The Engineer / Technical Buyer
Process Engineer, Design Engineer, Quality Engineer

What They Search For

  • Specific material grades, tolerances, and technical specs
  • Process capabilities (5-axis, EDM, tight-tolerance machining)
  • Certifications (ISO 9001, AS9100, ITAR, ASTM)

What Influences Their Decision

  • Does this vendor understand what I need?
  • Are their specs and capabilities clearly stated?
  • Do they have quality documentation and certs?

Content That Works

  • Spec sheets, capability lists, tolerance tables
  • Technical blog content, process explanations
  • Case studies with actual project details
📋
The Procurement Officer
Purchasing Manager, Supply Chain Manager, Procurement Director

What They Search For

  • Multiple vendors to compare pricing and lead time
  • In-stock availability and MOQ information
  • Distribution capability and geographic reach

What Influences Their Decision

  • Reliability — can this vendor consistently deliver on time?
  • Pricing and volume discounts
  • Past experience with this supplier or similar vendors

Content That Works

  • Product catalogs, quick quote functionality
  • Lead time and availability messaging
  • Established vendor relationships, testimonials
🏭
The Plant / Operations Manager
Plant Manager, Operations Manager, Maintenance Director

What They Search For

  • Urgency solutions — "we need this now" searches
  • MRO (Maintenance, Repair & Operations) supplies
  • Replacement parts and emergency inventory

What Influences Their Decision

  • Speed — can they get it here before my line goes down?
  • Local or regional availability
  • Trusted, established relationship with vendor

Content That Works

  • Emergency/rush order messaging front and center
  • In-stock, same-day shipping claims
  • Clear phone numbers and fast response times
👔
The Business Owner / Executive
Owner, VP of Manufacturing, CEO of SMB

What They Search For

  • Strategic supplier partnerships and long-term value
  • Company reputation, reviews, and longevity
  • Cost savings and efficiency improvements

What Influences Their Decision

  • Trust — do they look credible and established?
  • Total cost of partnership, not just unit price
  • Can this supplier scale with us?

Content That Works

  • About page, company story, years in business
  • Customer testimonials and case studies
  • Thought leadership content, industry recognition
Key Insight

Industrial buyers search with intent, not curiosity. When someone searches "A36 steel plate distributor Ohio" or "ISO 9001 certified aluminum machining" — they have a real, active, often urgent need. These are high-value searches that deserve precise, spec-matched content. Unlike B2C, there's rarely a "browsing" phase. They're ready to buy or close to it.

Section 6

Manufacturers, Distributors & Suppliers — Know Your Client

Before you build a single keyword or write a single piece of content — know what your client does and who they sell to. This changes everything about your strategy.

The Industrial Supply Chain

Each layer has a different customer, a different value proposition, and a different marketing strategy — even if they're all involved with the same product.

Raw Material Source
Mines, mills, and extractors of primary inputs
Supplier
Provides raw materials and components upstream
Manufacturer
Makes the finished product or component
Distributor
Stocks and resells to end users
End User
Uses the product in their operation

🏭 Manufacturer

Makes the product

What They Do

  • Create products from raw materials or components
  • Often have proprietary processes, certifications, or patents
  • May offer custom fabrication or standard stock

Who They Sell To

  • Distributors (channel/wholesale)
  • Direct to large end users or OEMs
  • Sometimes both simultaneously

Marketing Priorities

  • Brand credibility & product authority
  • Technical specs, certifications, case studies
  • "Why our product is best" messaging

📦 Distributor

Stocks & resells product

What They Do

  • Purchase inventory from manufacturers
  • Stock products in warehouses for quick fulfillment
  • Handle logistics, customer service, account mgmt

Who They Sell To

  • Contractors, fabricators, manufacturers, end users
  • Anyone who needs the product fast without buying direct

Marketing Priorities

  • Availability & lead time messaging
  • Breadth of product offering
  • "We have it in stock" is a primary differentiator

🔗 Supplier

Provides raw inputs upstream

What They Do

  • Provide raw materials or sub-components to manufacturers
  • Value = material quality, compliance, volume pricing
  • The term is sometimes used loosely — always clarify

Who They Sell To

  • Manufacturers and distributors
  • Sometimes government / military / large institutions

Marketing Priorities

  • Material grades, specs, compliance documentation
  • Volume pricing and MOQ information
  • Reliability, certifications, sourcing traceability

The B-SMART® Method

OBx's proprietary industrial keyword strategy framework. B-SMART gives you a structured way to think about how buyers search — and applies equally to SEO content strategy and PPC campaign structure. The nuance: in PPC, budget is attached to every keyword, so relevancy is even more critical.

B
Brand
Manufacturer brands, proprietary product names, or the client's own brand
S
Size / Shape / SKU
Dimensions, product forms, configurations, and SKU identifiers
M
Material
Material type, grade, alloy designation, or composition
A
Application / Industry / Area
Use case, industry served, or geographic market
R
Requirement
Certifications, compliance standards, performance specs
T
Type
Process type, product category, project classification
How B-SMART Works

B-SMART isn't a fill-in-all-six template for every keyword. It's a lens — a way of thinking about the full universe of how a buyer might search. Some keywords will hit 2–3 dimensions; others might use all 6. The goal is to systematically identify how buyers describe what they need, not how your client describes what they sell. Those are often different things.

Industrial Terminology Wins

Using the right language is a trust signal. Using the wrong language — even casually — will undermine credibility with experienced buyers. These tables are your reference guide.

1. Singular vs. Plural — Product Form Variations

Misused (Avoid)Correct / PreferredWhy It Matters
"Plates""Plate""Plate" is used as a material category (e.g., "steel plate")
"Sheets""Sheet"Used generically as a product form, not individual pieces
"Tubes""Tubing" or "Tube""Tubing" refers to the product form; "tubes" sounds consumer-facing
"Bars""Bar""Bar stock" or "bar" is the industry norm
"Rods""Rod""Rod" is the standard form reference
"Pipes""Pipe""Pipe" used generically; "pipes" sounds consumer-facing
"Wires""Wire""Wire" or "wire rod" is preferred
"Coils""Coil"Refers to the product form, not individual pieces

2. Commonly Confused Product Terms

Misused / ConfusedCorrect Distinction
Tube vs. PipeNot interchangeable. Tube is measured by OD + wall thickness; Pipe is measured by nominal pipe size (NPS) + schedule.
Sheet vs. PlateGenerally, sheet = under 3/16" thick; plate = 3/16" or thicker (varies by material)
Bar vs. RodRod is typically round and longer/coiled; Bar can be flat, square, round, or hex — broader term
Square Bar vs. Square TubeSquare bar is solid; square tube is hollow — critical distinction for load-bearing applications
Round Bar vs. ShaftShaft implies precision-ground tolerances; round bar is standard mill finish
Strip vs. Sheet vs. CoilStrip = narrow, coiled; Sheet = cut flat panels; Coil = the rolled, uncut form
Structural Tubing vs. Mechanical TubingStructural = for construction/load-bearing; Mechanical = for precision applications, tighter tolerances

3. Material & Alloy Terminology

MisusedClarification
"Stainless"Should be "stainless steel" in formal and B2B contexts
"Aluminum" vs. "Aluminium"Both correct — "Aluminum" is the US standard; "Aluminium" is international
"Mild Steel"Technically correct but often confused with "carbon steel" — mild steel IS a low-carbon steel
"Alloy" (generic)Should specify the alloy grade (e.g., 6061 aluminum, A36 steel, 304 stainless)
"Galvanized" vs. "Galvannealed"Different coating processes — not the same finish or application
"304 vs. 316 Stainless"Frequently swapped — 316 has molybdenum for better corrosion resistance

4. Measurement & Specification

MisusedClarification
Gauge vs. ThicknessGauge is a legacy measurement system — not a direct inch/mm value; always verify the actual decimal equivalent
OD vs. IDOutside Diameter vs. Inside Diameter — critical for tube and pipe ordering
Wall vs. ScheduleFor pipe: schedule (Sch 40, Sch 80) defines wall thickness; for tube: wall is stated directly
Nominal Size vs. Actual SizeNominal pipe/lumber sizes do not equal actual dimensions
"1/4 inch plate"Should specify grade — A36, A572, AR plate — grade matters as much as thickness

5. Quantity & Usage Terms

TermWhat It MeansCommonly Confused WithWhy It Matters
EAU (Estimated Annual Usage)Total projected quantity needed over 12 monthsEAQ, EOQ, or "annual order"Used by mills and distributors to determine pricing tiers and stocking commitments
EOQ (Economic Order Quantity)Optimal order quantity that minimizes inventory and ordering costsMOQ — buyers often confuse theseEOQ is internally calculated; MOQ is supplier-imposed
MOQ (Minimum Order Quantity)The minimum a supplier will accept per orderEOQ or standard order quantityMisunderstanding MOQ can delay procurement or result in over-ordering
MRO (Maintenance, Repair & Operations)Supplies used in operations but not in the final productDirect materials or production stockDifferent procurement cycle, different buyer, different urgency

6. Condition & Finishes

MisusedCorrect Distinction
Hot Rolled vs. Cold RolledHR has mill scale, looser tolerances; CR is smoother, tighter dimensions — not interchangeable
Cold Rolled vs. Cold DrawnCold rolled = flat product; Cold drawn = bar/tube pulled through a die for tighter tolerance
Annealed vs. TemperedBoth are heat treatments but with opposite hardness outcomes — annealed softens; tempered hardens
"Polished"Should specify grit or finish type (e.g., #4 brushed, #8 mirror) — "polished" alone is vague
Mill Finish vs. No. 1 FinishSpecific stainless finish grades — often used loosely or incorrectly
Section 10 — New

Questions to Ask Your Client — Intake Cheat Sheet

When you get a new industrial or B2B client, these are the questions that will unlock everything you need to build smart strategy.

🏭Business & Model
  • QAre you a manufacturer, distributor, supplier, or some combination?
  • QWhat do you make or sell — and what does the finished product/service look like?
  • QDo you sell direct to end users, through channel partners, or both?
  • QWhat industries or verticals do you primarily serve?
  • QWhat is your geographic reach — local, regional, or national?
👥Buyers & ICP
  • QWho is your ideal customer — what does their company look like? What's their title?
  • QWho actually makes the purchase decision — and who influences it?
  • QWhat is a typical order value, and how frequently do customers reorder?
  • QWhat is the LTV of your best customers?
  • QWhat triggers someone to search for you — urgency, planned purchase, new project?
🔧Products & Capabilities
  • QWhat are your top 3–5 products or service lines by revenue?
  • QDo you offer custom or standard products — or both?
  • QWhat certifications, standards, or compliance requirements do you meet?
  • QWhat materials, grades, or specifications are most commonly requested?
  • QWhat is your lead time, and how does that compare to competitors?
🏆Differentiators & Competition
  • QWhy do customers choose you over a competitor?
  • QWho are your top 2–3 competitors, and how do you typically compare?
  • QHave you lost business to a competitor recently — why did the customer go elsewhere?
  • QWhat do your best customers say about you in reviews or testimonials?
  • QWhat do customers ask for that you can't currently offer?
Pro Tip

Many industrial clients won't have polished answers to these questions right away. Use the conversation to build the picture together. The exercise itself creates value and positions you as a strategic partner, not just a vendor.

Section 11 — New

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the patterns that show up most often when people are new to this vertical. Knowing them in advance puts you ahead.

✗ Don't Do This

Defaulting to hyper-local targeting for distributors. "Ohio steel plate distributor" is often too limiting. Most industrial distributors ship regionally or nationally. Assume national reach until confirmed otherwise.

✓ Do This Instead

Ask the client explicitly about their shipping footprint. Build targeting that matches their actual reach — which is often much broader than they initially say.

✗ Don't Do This

Writing copy that sounds consumer-facing. "Check out our wide selection of steel pipes!" uses casual, B2C language. "Steel pipe" not "pipes." No exclamation points. Precision language only.

✓ Do This Instead

Use the terminology tables in this training. Reference grades, certifications, and specifications. Buyers in this space expect technical precision — it builds trust instantly.

✗ Don't Do This

Ignoring certifications in keyword and content strategy. Many industrial buyers filter vendors by certifications first. If your client has them and you're not using them, you're leaving significant opportunity on the table.

✓ Do This Instead

Make certifications prominent — in meta titles, headers, landing pages, and PPC ads. "ISO 9001 Certified CNC Machining" is a keyword AND a trust signal.

✗ Don't Do This

Treating a manufacturer's strategy the same as a distributor's. A distributor's homepage should lead with availability and speed. A manufacturer's should lead with capability and quality.

✓ Do This Instead

Always identify which layer of the supply chain your client occupies before any strategy work. Use the side-by-side table in Section 6 to guide your approach.

✗ Don't Do This

Using generic material references without grades. "Aluminum machining" is weaker than "6061 aluminum machining." Grades are how buyers think and search — especially engineers and procurement teams.

✓ Do This Instead

Always push for specific grades during intake. If the client isn't sure which grades to highlight, look at what competitors rank for and what their own product pages feature most prominently.

✗ Don't Do This

Expecting quick conversions and measuring success like B2C. A 90-day sales cycle means your content may generate a lead today that doesn't close for months.

✓ Do This Instead

Set client expectations around lead quality and sales cycle length. Focus on form fills, RFQ submissions, phone calls, and lead quality — not just volume. One qualified B2B lead can be worth more than 1,000 B2C transactions.

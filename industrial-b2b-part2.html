Section 1 — The Foundation

Intent Over Volume: The Industrial SEO Mindset

Before you touch a keyword tool or write a single title tag, you need to rewire how you think about SEO success in this vertical. The rules are genuinely different here.

🧠 Core Principle — From OBx Strategy Guide

Search volume is NOT the primary decision driver for industrial B2B. A keyword with 10 monthly searches that represents a $200,000 contract is infinitely more valuable than a keyword with 10,000 searches from students writing papers. Optimize for material-, grade-, and form-based intent. The query defines the buyer's need.

Why Industrial Search Is Different

  • Buyers search with precision — they already know what they need
  • Specificity = intent = purchase readiness
  • Generic terms are almost never searched by actual B2B buyers
  • The same product can have dozens of high-value, low-volume keyword variants
  • One converting keyword can justify an entire content investment

High Intent vs. Non-Intent — Know the Difference

✅ Target These — High Intent❌ Avoid These — Non-IntentWhy the Distinction Matters
"17-4PH stainless bar stock""steel bar"Too generic — could be DIY, automotive hobby, or student research
"DFARS compliant stainless steel plate""stainless steel"No specification = no intent signal; attracts wrong audience
"A36 steel plate cut to length Ohio""steel plates"Plural form alone signals consumer-facing context
"6061-T6 aluminum sheet distributor""aluminum sheet"No grade, no intent — could be craft/hobby, not procurement
"AS9100 CNC machining aerospace""machining"Too broad — doesn't indicate buyer type, material, or application
"304L stainless tubing food grade""stainless tubing"Missing grade and application = missing the buyer's filter
Scope Rule — Always Apply This

Only optimize for what the client actually sells. Never introduce services or products they don't offer — even if the keywords look attractive. Stay in scope. This protects the client's credibility and your strategy's conversion rate.

Section 2

The 4 Industrial Content Page Types

Industrial websites need four distinct types of pages to capture the full spectrum of how buyers search. Each type serves a different search intent and a different buyer in the process.

📂
Type 1 — Category Page
Broad material or service grouping
What it is

The top-level page that represents an entire product or service category. Acts as a hub and establishes topical authority.

Who lands here
  • Early-stage buyers researching a material category
  • Engineers exploring product form options
  • Procurement managers confirming a vendor carries a product line
Example URLs

Distributor:

/steel-plate/

Manufacturer:

/aluminum-fabrication/
SEO priority

Breadth of grades and forms. Internal links to all subcategory pages. Strong authority signals.

🔬
Type 2 — Form / Product Page
Specific grade, form, or SKU
What it is

The highest-intent page type. Targets buyers who know exactly what they need — specific grade, form, and often dimensions. This is where most conversions happen.

Who lands here
  • Engineers with an active spec requirement
  • Procurement teams with a PO in hand
  • Plant managers searching for a specific product fast
Example URLs

Distributor:

/a36-steel-plate/

Manufacturer:

/6061-aluminum-machining/
SEO priority

Exact grade naming. Spec tables. Cert information. Availability or capability messaging. This is where B-SMART does the most work.

🏗️
Type 3 — Application / Industry Page
Use case or vertical-specific content
What it is

Pages that target buyers who search by what they're building or which industry they're in — not just the material itself. Captures the "A" in B-SMART.

Who lands here
  • Engineers searching for solutions to a specific application
  • Buyers searching by industry ("aerospace aluminum supplier")
  • OEMs or integrators looking for validated materials
Example URLs

Manufacturer:

/aerospace-aluminum-machining/

Distributor:

/steel-for-structural-fabrication/
SEO priority

Industry-specific language. Relevant grades for that application. Case studies or project examples where possible.

📋
Type 4 — Compliance / Certification Page
Requirement-driven content
What it is

Pages targeting buyers who filter by certifications, standards, or compliance requirements before anything else. Captures the "R" in B-SMART. Often overlooked — and often high-converting.

Who lands here
  • Defense/government procurement requiring DFARS compliance
  • Medical device buyers requiring biocompatibility certs
  • Quality engineers searching for ISO/ASTM-compliant suppliers
Example URLs

Manufacturer:

/iso-9001-certified-machining/

Distributor:

/dfars-compliant-stainless-steel/
SEO priority

Exact certification names in title and H1. Documentation language. Trust signals (cert logos, downloadable certs).

How These Pages Work Together

These four page types form a hierarchy: Category → Form/Product → Application → Compliance. Internal links should flow naturally between them. A user landing on an A36 Steel Plate page (Type 2) should be able to navigate to structural fabrication applications (Type 3) and ASTM-certified supply (Type 4). Google rewards this interconnected architecture. So do buyers.

Layering Keywords Across Page Types

Start with a Material + Type category page ("Carbon Steel Plate"), then build out Material + Type + Size form pages ("A36 Steel Plate"), then add an Application page ("Carbon Steel for Structural Fabrication"), then a Compliance page ("ASTM Certified Carbon Steel Plate"). That's four high-intent pages from one product, each targeting a different point in the buyer's search journey.

Section 4

On-Page SEO for Industrial Clients

The terminology work from Part 1 pays off here. On-page SEO for industrial buyers is about precision — exact grades, exact terms, exact intent. Here's how each element should be treated.

On-Page Element Guide — Industrial B2B
Title Tag

Lead with the primary keyword. Include grade, form, and a differentiator. Under 60 characters.

✓ Good:A36 Steel Plate | Cut to Length | O'Neal Steel
✓ Good:6061 Aluminum Machining | AS9100 Certified | Bison Profab
✗ Weak:Steel Products | Industrial Supplier | [Client Name]
H1 Tag

One per page. Should match the primary search intent exactly — never generic.

✓ Good:A36 Steel Plate — In Stock, Cut to Length
✗ Weak:Our Steel Products
Meta Description

Lead with the differentiator. Include the grade/form. Mention the CTA. 150–160 characters max.

✓ Good:A36 carbon steel plate in stock. ASTM certified, custom cut to length. Request a quote or call for same-day availability.
✗ Weak:We offer high quality steel products for all your industrial needs. Contact us today to learn more.
H2 / H3 Subheads

Use subheads to organize content AND capture secondary keyword intent. Common strong subheads for industrial pages:

A36 Steel Plate SpecificationsAvailable Sizes & ThicknessesMill Certifications & StandardsCustom Cutting & ProcessingCommon Applications
Body Copy

Write with the buyer's language — grades, certs, forms. Avoid marketing fluff. Industrial buyers skim for specs. Lead with availability, certifications, capabilities. Include a spec table wherever possible.

Golden rule: If a buyer read this page and still couldn't determine if this client sells what they need — the page isn't specific enough.

CTA Placement

Industrial buyers are action-oriented once they've confirmed you have what they need.

✓ Strong CTAs:Request a Quote · Get Pricing · Check Availability · Submit an RFQ
✗ Weak CTAs:Learn More · Contact Us · Click Here
URL Structure

URLs should mirror the content hierarchy and include the primary keyword.

✓ Good:/steel/carbon-steel/a36-steel-plate/
✗ Weak:/products/cat12/item-8847-v2/
Industrial Shorthand Is Valid

Don't always force the full formal name into every keyword. Industrial buyers frequently use shorthand — "304H plate", "2205 duplex", "17-4PH bar". Validate with keyword research — both forms often have search volume.

Section 5

Technical SEO Considerations for Industrial Sites

Industrial websites come with their own technical quirks. Large product catalogs, spec sheet PDFs, and legacy site structures are common. Here's what to prioritize.

📊

Spec Tables & PDFs

  • Spec tables in HTML are crawlable and rank independently — don't rely on PDFs only
  • If a client has mill cert PDFs, link to them — they build trust AND authority
  • Structure spec tables with clear headers: Grade · Form · Size · Availability
🔗

Internal Linking Strategy

  • Category pages should link to all Form/Product pages beneath them
  • Form pages should link to relevant Application pages
  • Compliance pages should link to every applicable product they cover
  • Avoid orphaned product pages — they won't rank
🗂️

Large Catalog Management

  • Prioritize high-revenue SKUs and categories for optimization first
  • Use canonical tags on near-duplicate product pages (same product, different sizes)
  • Don't create thin pages for every single SKU — consolidate when appropriate
📐

Schema Markup

  • Product schema for form/product pages — include name, description, availability
  • Organization schema with certifications and accreditations
  • LocalBusiness schema if client has regional distribution presence
  • FAQ schema on compliance and technical spec pages
⚡

Page Speed

  • Industrial sites often have heavy imagery of equipment and facilities
  • Compress product/facility images without losing quality
  • B2B buyers are often on desktop — but mobile matters for field buyers
🔍

Crawlability

  • Ensure product catalog pages are crawlable — not blocked in robots.txt
  • Many legacy industrial sites have URL parameters that create duplicate content
  • Check for session IDs, filters, and sort parameters in URLs
  • Submit an XML sitemap that prioritizes key product and category pages
Section 6 — Apply Before Every Decision

The Quick Decision Framework

Before adding any keyword, any page, or any section — run it through these four questions. This comes directly from OBx's internal industrial strategy guide.

1

Does the client actually sell this?

Never optimize for products, materials, or services the client doesn't offer — even if there's strong search volume. It wastes budget, brings in leads they can't convert, and damages credibility.

If No → Exclude completely
2

Would their ICP actually search this way?

A keyword might be relevant to the industry but not how the actual buyer searches. Cross-reference with how engineers, procurement officers, or plant managers actually phrase their queries.

If No → Deprioritize or reformulate
3

Is this specific enough to indicate purchase intent?

Generic = low intent = wrong audience. If the keyword doesn't include a grade, form, certification, application, or specification, it's almost certainly too broad for industrial B2B.

If No → Add specificity (apply B-SMART layers)
4

Are we drifting into services or value-adds they don't offer?

A distributor that mentions "custom fabrication" will attract leads they can't fulfill. A machining shop that mentions "raw material supply" will confuse their ICP. Scope creep in content strategy is a credibility killer.

If Yes → Pull back and reframe
When All 4 Pass

Only when a keyword or content piece passes all four checks should it move forward into your strategy. In industrial B2B, one qualified lead beats ten thousand unqualified visitors.

Section 8

Common Industrial SEO Mistakes to Avoid

✗ Chasing Volume Over Intent

Building a strategy around "steel" (high volume) instead of "A36 steel plate distributor Ohio" (low volume, high conversion). Volume metrics mislead in this vertical.

✓ Prioritize by Intent and LTV

Evaluate keywords by who's searching and what they're worth when they convert. Build the business case: 30 searches/month × 5% conversion × $50K average order = significant pipeline.

✗ Creating Generic Category Pages Only

A "Steel Products" page that lists everything the client sells with no grade-specific depth. Buyers searching for "A36 plate" won't find this satisfying — and Google won't rank it over pages that actually address the specific search.

✓ Build Down to Grade-Level Pages

Category page → Material subpage → Grade/form page. Each level captures more specific intent. Grade-level pages are where most conversions happen. Build category pages as navigation hubs and spec pages as the destination.

✗ Ignoring Compliance/Cert Pages

Not creating certification-specific content because it seems niche. DFARS, ISO, AS9100, ITAR — these are filters that eliminate most competitors. Buyers who search for "DFARS compliant titanium" are highly qualified and ready to procure.

✓ Build Compliance Pages Proactively

If the client has any certifications — build pages for them. These pages have strong conversion intent, limited competition, and establish authority in niche markets that can be extremely high-value.

✗ Writing Copy for a General Audience

"We offer a wide range of high-quality metal products for all your industrial needs." This says nothing. An engineer reading this has no idea if you carry the grade they need or the cert they require.

✓ Write for the Specific Buyer

Use the buyer personas from Part 1. Write for the engineer who needs specs. Write for the procurement officer who needs availability and lead time. Be specific or be ignored.

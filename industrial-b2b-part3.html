Core Principle

Section 2

Why Industrial PPC Is Uniquely High-Stakes

PPC in industrial B2B is not the same game as eCommerce or local services. The economics, search behavior, and margin for error are completely different. Understand this before you touch a single bid.

💰

High CPCs, Low Volume

Industrial keywords often cost $8–$30+ per click. Search volumes are small. There's no room for irrelevant traffic. Every click must be defensible with intent and a clear connection to pipeline.

🎯

Specificity = ROI

The more specific the keyword, the more qualified the click, the higher the conversion potential. "Steel bar" wastes budget. "A36 hot rolled steel bar Ohio distributor" spends money to attract a real buyer.

📈

The LTV Math Changes Everything

One converted B2B lead can be worth $50,000–$500,000 in lifetime revenue. This fundamentally changes how you evaluate CPC and what you're willing to spend per conversion. Always contextualize CPL against LTV.

Section 3

The Keyword Research Process — Building From Scratch

Before you launch anything, you need a keyword list built on intent. Here's the step-by-step process OBx follows — from brainstorming through refinement — using B-SMART as the core framework.

1

Brainstorming & Intent — Start With the Business

Identify the client's core services or products first. Then focus exclusively on transactional keywords — terms that show a user is ready to buy or request a quote (e.g., "A36 steel plate distributor Ohio"), not informational keywords (e.g., "what is A36 steel"). In industrial PPC, informational traffic is a budget leak.

Apply B-SMART here: Run each dimension (Brand, Size, Material, Application, Requirement, Type) and brainstorm keyword variations for each. This is how you build a comprehensive list without guessing.
2

Tool & SERP-Based Research

Use OBx Chat to expand your initial list and gather data. Use Google Keyword Planner to validate search volume and identify variations. Use the SERP itself — look at what Google suggests, what competitors are bidding on, and what "People Also Ask" questions reveal about buyer language.

OBx Chat tip: Prompt it with the client's business type, products, and ICP. Ask it to generate keyword variations using B-SMART dimensions. Then validate and refine — don't take the output raw.
3

List Refinement & Organization

Organize keywords into tightly themed ad groups by product or capability (e.g., one ad group for "A36 plate," a separate one for "304 stainless sheet"). The ideal keyword length is 2 to 4 words — specific enough to show intent, not so long they have zero volume.

Cleanup checklist: Run a spell check · Use Excel's "Remove Duplicates" · Sort alphabetically · Remove anything under 2 words or over 5 words · Cross-check against B-SMART — are all dimensions covered?
OBx Formatting Rules — Keyword Lists

Use singular nouns for most industrial keywords — "Bar" not "Bars," "Coil" not "Coils," "Plate" not "Plates." Exceptions: words where the plural form is also a verb, like "Piping" or "Tubing." You'll build out plural terms later in the ad copy creation process, not in the keyword list itself.

Section 4 — OBx Policies

OBx Bidding Policies — Know These Before You Build

These are not suggestions — they are OBx policies and standards that apply to every account. Know them before you build your first keyword list.

🚫 Competitor Business Names

OBx does not bid on competitor business names. This is company policy and applies across all accounts and all verticals. If a client asks you to bid on a competitor's brand name — that's a conversation to escalate, not something you do unilaterally.

This is separate from bidding on competitor's product names when a distributor carries the same product — that's permitted and often effective for distributors.

📋 Branded Terms — When to Bid

Bid on a client's own brand name only when one of these conditions applies:

  • They are a manufacturer whose products are sold by distributors (defend your brand)
  • They are a distributor of multiple brands (brand campaigns are high performers)
  • They do not rank organically for their own brand name

If the client dominates organic results for their own name, branded PPC spend may not be the best use of budget. Evaluate case by case.

✅ Match Type Defaults by Platform

OBx match type defaults differ by platform:

  • Google → Broad Match (with strong negatives in place)
  • Bing → Phrase Match
  • Exact Match → Rarely, only when absolutely necessary for a specific high-value term

This may feel counterintuitive if you've learned to default to phrase match everywhere. The reasoning: Google's algorithm has matured significantly, and broad match with proper negatives often outperforms tight match types when conversion data is available.

Why Broad Match on Google — The Nuance

Google's broad match has evolved. When paired with a mature negative keyword list and a well-structured account with conversion data, broad match allows Google's algorithm to find converting queries you wouldn't have thought to target. The keyword you bid on is a signal to Google — not a rigid filter. The negative keyword list is what provides the filter. This is why negatives must be built before launch, not after. Broad match without negatives is wasted spend. Broad match with comprehensive negatives is a discovery engine.

Section 6 — The Diagnostic Process

How to Conduct an Industrial PPC Account Review

A PPC account review is not a glance at impressions and clicks. It's a systematic diagnosis that follows a specific sequence. Skip steps at your own risk — and your client's budget.

1

Verify Conversion Tracking — Do This First, Every Time

Without accurate conversion tracking, it's impossible to measure success or make data-driven decisions. You need to know which keywords, ads, and campaigns are driving valuable actions.

  • Navigate to Goals → Conversions → Summary in Google Ads
  • Identify what is being tracked as a conversion — is it a form fill, a phone call, a purchase, or a page visit?
  • Confirm that conversion actions align directly with the client's actual business goals
  • Check that conversions are firing — a conversion action that exists but isn't recording data is a red flag
⚠️ Never optimize an account without confirming tracking is working correctly first
2

Analyze Campaign Structure & Budget Allocation

This step helps you understand how the budget is distributed and whether the structure is set up for success. Limited budgets require a focused strategy — not fragmented spend across too many campaigns.

  • Navigate to the Campaigns tab and count the number of active campaigns
  • Review the bidding strategy: Is it automated (Maximize Conversions, Target CPA) or manual CPC?
  • Assess whether the structure is logical — segmented by product, service, or location for better control and reporting
  • Identify any campaigns that are limited by budget — these are opportunities being left on the table
Best Practice: Structure should mirror how the client's sales team thinks about their products — not how it was convenient to set up at launch
3

Analyze the Bid-to-Budget Ratio

The relationship between daily budget and average CPC determines how many opportunities (clicks) a client gets per day. A poor ratio severely limits exposure and lead potential. Use this formula before making any bid changes.

Daily Budget ÷ Average CPC = Clicks Per Day

⚠️ Increasing Bids on Limited Budget

Bid increases from $1.45 → $2.00 on a $15 daily budget

Clicks drop from 10 → 7
Fewer opportunities for leads

✅ Decreasing Bids on Limited Budget

Reducing avg. CPC from $5.00 → $1.00 on a $10 budget

Clicks increase from 2 → 10
5x the opportunities

📈 Increasing the Budget

Client increases budget from $20 → $40, same bid

Clicks double from 10 → 20
Double the opportunities

Before making any bid or budget changes, always ask: What data is this decision based on? And: How will this change impact the client's lead quantity and revenue?

4

Review Keywords & Budget Distribution

Often, a small number of keywords consume the majority of the budget. We need to confirm that the high-cost keywords are also high-performing and show strong intent — not just high-spending.

  • Navigate to Keywords → Search Keywords
  • Sort the keyword list by "Cost" in descending order
  • Analyze the top-spending keywords — are they directly relevant to the client's core offerings?
  • If a high-spending keyword has low conversion rate, investigate before cutting — it may be a landing page or offer problem, not a keyword problem
The goal: confirm that money is going where intent is highest — not where history has made it habit to spend
Section 7 — Critical Ongoing Practice

The Search Terms Report — Your Most Honest Data Source

The search terms report shows the actual queries people typed into Google that triggered your ads. This is the ultimate test of keyword relevance and quality. Review it weekly — not monthly.

Where to Find It

Navigate to Keywords → Search Terms in Google Ads. Sort by Cost descending. Look at what people actually typed — not what you bid on. These are often two very different things, especially early in an account's life or when using broad match.

How to Evaluate and Act on What You See

✅ Good Query Signals ❌ Bad Query Signals What to Do
"5 hp industrial water pump" — specific, transactional, matches product "how does a pump work" — informational, not buying Add "how does" as a broad match negative. Keep the specific term.
"304 stainless plate distributor Ohio" — grade + form + geography "stainless steel wall tiles" — consumer application, wrong context Add "tiles" as a negative. The grade query shows buyer intent.
"DFARS compliant titanium bar" — specific requirement, high-value buyer "prescription glasses" triggered by "industrial glasses safety" — completely off Add "prescription" and "eyewear" as negatives. Investigate which keyword triggered this.
"A36 plate cut to length Houston" — specific, geographic, transactional "metal working DIY projects" — hobbyist intent Add "DIY" and "projects" as broad match negatives. These will eliminate dozens of variants.
"ISO 9001 machine shop near me" — certification + proximity "machine shop jobs near me" — career intent, not buyer Add "jobs," "careers," "hiring" as broad match negatives from the start.

If a Query Is Irrelevant → Add as Negative

Add irrelevant queries as negative keywords to prevent wasting money on them in the future. The STR is your feedback mechanism — what the account is actually doing vs. what you intended it to do.

  • Use broad match negatives for single-word themes (e.g., "DIY," "jobs," "free")
  • Use phrase match negatives for multi-word phrases (e.g., "Home Depot," "how to build")
  • Use exact match negatives for very specific queries you never want to trigger (e.g., [Fiber Optic])

If a Query Is Highly Relevant → Add as a Keyword

If you see a search query that converts well but isn't in your keyword list, add it as a new keyword so you can control bids on it directly and get more bidding precision.

  • Add as phrase match on Bing or if you need reach control on Google
  • Add to the most relevant ad group — not just the catch-all campaign
  • This is how accounts improve over time — the STR is your gold mine
⚠️ Avoid "Chasing With Negatives"

This happens when you're constantly adding negative keywords because your primary keywords are too broad. If you find yourself adding 20+ negatives per week, it's often a signal to step back and refine the keyword match types themselves rather than playing whack-a-mole with negatives. The root cause is usually a keyword that's doing too much work for too many irrelevant queries.

Section 8 — Build Before Launch

Negative Keyword Strategy: Protecting Your Budget

What you block is just as important as what you target. Build negatives before you launch — not after you've spent money on the wrong audience. The OBx Informational Terms Master List is your starting point.

Start Here — OBx Master List

Review the list and remove any terms that might actually apply to what you're targeting. Negative Match Type Strategy

Use the right match type for each negative: Broad match for single-word negatives (e.g., "fidget," "DIY") — this eliminates the entire theme across all variations. Phrase match for multi-word phrases you want to exclude (e.g., "Home Depot," "how to build"). Exact match to exclude a very specific query you never want to trigger (e.g., [Fiber Optic]). One well-placed broad match negative does the work of dozens of specific ones.

Section 9

Ad Copy That Works for Industrial Buyers

Industrial buyers respond to specificity, credibility, and speed. They are not browsing — they have a real need. Your ad copy needs to confirm immediately that you can meet that need. Match the keyword. Speak to the buyer. Lead with what they care about.

✅ What Works

  • Lead with the grade or capability in Headline 1 — match the keyword exactly
  • Use certifications as a headline — "AS9100 Certified," "ISO 9001," "DFARS Compliant"
  • Mention availability if a distributor: "In Stock," "Ready to Ship," "Same-Day Cut Available"
  • Use specific, action-oriented CTAs: "Request a Quote," "Get Pricing," "Submit an RFQ"
  • Include technical capabilities that signal expertise: "Mill Certified," "Tight Tolerance Machining"
  • Name specific materials or grades in the ad — it signals relevance to a buyer scanning fast

❌ What Doesn't Work

  • Generic openers: "Welcome to [Company]" or "We Are Your Trusted Partner..."
  • Consumer language: "Amazing Deals," "Best Prices," "Shop Now"
  • Vague claims: "High Quality Products," "Great Service," "Wide Selection"
  • "Contact Us Today" as the only CTA — too passive for a buyer with an active need
  • Headline 1 that doesn't match the keyword — disconnects the buyer and kills Quality Score
  • No grade or capability mentioned — buyers scan for their specific spec in half a second

Ad Extensions — Don't Skip These

Sitelink extensions should link to your highest-converting page types: specific grade pages, certification pages, quote forms, and catalog pages. Callout extensions are your differentiator space: "Mill Certified Stock" · "Same-Day Cut to Length" · "AS9100 Certified" · "National Shipping" · "RFQ Response in 24 Hours". Structured snippets can feature specific grades carried, capabilities, or industries served. These cost nothing extra and significantly increase CTR from qualified buyers.

Section 10 — Critical Partnership

PPC + SEO Synergy: You Are a Team

A user seeing a business in both paid and organic search results is more likely to trust and click on that brand. PPC and SEO are not competing channels — they are complementary strategies that are stronger together than either is alone.

🔍 SEO
Organic, long-term, compounding
  • Builds authority and trust over time
  • Slower to rank but longer-lasting results
  • Content signals to Google what the site is about
  • Can surface for long-tail, specific grade queries
  • New pages need time to gain visibility in search
⚡
💰 PPC
Paid, immediate, controllable
  • Immediate visibility — ads go live same day
  • Drives traffic to new pages before SEO kicks in
  • Search Terms Report reveals real buyer language
  • Can cover gaps where organic rankings are weak
  • Budget can be scaled up or down in real time

How to Work Together — Practical Actions

🔄

Feed STR Data Into SEO Strategy

Use the PPC Search Terms Report to discover new, high-intent keywords that can be targeted in SEO content strategy. If buyers are searching for "DFARS compliant titanium bar" and converting through PPC — that's a content brief for the SEO team.

📄

Run PPC to New SEO Pages Immediately

If the SEO team implements a new page or new content, run ads to that page right away — as long as there's budget and it's a client priority. PPC is faster and will direct traffic to the page in a much shorter time frame than organic rankings alone.

🛡️

Cover Organic Gaps With Paid

If organic rankings are low for a high-value keyword, use paid search to ensure immediate visibility. This is the core logic behind integrated marketing — wherever PPC lacks, SEO can make up for it, and vice versa.

From the Field — Paid Media Team
Your SEO counterpart will be your best and most frequent collaborator. You should be building strategy together because wherever PPC lacks, SEO can make up for it — and vice versa. If your SEO implements a new page, have ads running to it. If your keyword research reveals new intent themes, share them with your SEO. Consistent message and user experience between your ads and the corresponding landing pages isn't just good practice — it's what makes the whole thing work as a unified strategy rather than two separate tactics running in parallel.

📊 SERP Domination — Why It Matters

  • Greater visibility — appearing in paid AND organic means your brand is seen twice in the results
  • Increased trust — multiple listings act as social proof, boosting credibility with the buyer
  • Strategic defense — occupying both spots prevents competitors from gaining that visibility
  • Message consistency — your paid ad and your organic listing should reinforce each other

🔍 What to Align Between PPC and SEO

  • Landing page message matches the ad copy — no disconnect when the buyer arrives
  • Keywords in ad copy match the H1 and title tag of the landing page
  • CTAs are consistent — if the ad says "Request a Quote," the page should lead with a quote form
  • Share keyword performance data both directions — what's converting in PPC is a signal for SEO priority
Section 11

Common Industrial PPC Mistakes to Avoid

These patterns consistently undermine industrial PPC performance. Knowing them before you launch is how you avoid them.

✗ Skipping Conversion Tracking Verification

Launching or optimizing an account without confirming that conversion tracking is firing correctly. Every optimization decision made without accurate conversion data is a guess — and guesses cost money in PPC.

✓ Verify Tracking Before Anything Else

Step 1 of every account review is conversion tracking verification. Check Goals → Conversions → Summary. Confirm what is being tracked and that it aligns with the client's actual business goals — not just whatever was convenient to set up at launch.

✗ Launching Without Negatives

Starting a campaign with no negative keyword list. In industrial B2B, broad match without negatives will immediately burn budget on consumer, educational, and career-intent searches — all the wrong audience at real CPC costs.

✓ Build Negatives Before Launch

Use the OBx Informational Terms Master List as your baseline. Layer in B-SMART-driven client-specific negatives. Use broad match negatives for themes (e.g., "DIY" eliminates dozens of irrelevant variants in one entry). Review STR weekly and expand from there.

✗ Ignoring the STR and Just Watching Metrics

Looking at impressions, clicks, and conversions at the campaign level without reviewing what actual search queries triggered the ads. The STR is where the account is actually performing — or failing.

✓ Review the STR Weekly

Identify irrelevant queries and add them as negatives. Identify high-converting queries that aren't in your keyword list and add them. The STR is both your quality filter and your discovery engine — don't skip it.

✗ Evaluating CPL Without LTV Context

Panicking about a $1,200 CPL when that lead could be worth $60,000 in lifetime revenue. Industrial CPL math is completely different from consumer PPC. Evaluating it without LTV leads to cutting spend that was actually working.

✓ Always Anchor CPL to LTV

Present performance in terms the client cares about: "We generated X leads this month at $Y CPL. Based on your average order value, even a 10% close rate represents $Z in potential pipeline." Change the frame entirely.

✗ PPC and SEO Working in Silos

Running PPC campaigns and SEO strategies completely independently — different keyword lists, different content priorities, no shared intelligence. This is leaving money on the table and creating inconsistent brand experiences for buyers.

✓ Build Strategy Together

Share STR data with your SEO counterpart. Run PPC to new SEO pages immediately. Use paid to cover organic gaps. Align landing page messaging with ad copy. You and your SEO are one integrated marketing team — not two separate services running simultaneously.
